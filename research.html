Job Market Paper

Environmental Protection, Interjurisdictional Transport, and Demographic Distribution of Solid Waste Disposal
Using the data of intercounty waste flows in California and a structural model to estimate a hauler's choice of where to dump waste, this paper studies the effects of environmental protection policies on the spatial and demographic distribution of solid waste. I find that waste is currently carted more to disposal facilities in high percent minority than in white communities. The disparity is eliminated for asians once I control for income, disposal fees, and trash transport distance, but persists for blacks and hispanics. Waste is sent more to facilities in black and hispanic communities although these facilities may be expensive and/or distant. Waste is also sent more black communities if it is intercounty flow. Hence, policies that decrease intercounty waste flows, i.e. out-of-county import bans, import taxes, and fuel taxes, would reduce the share of waste that ends up near black communities.

Publication

Auctioning the Digital Dividend: A Model for Spectrum Auctions , (with Toby Daglish and Yigit Saglam).
International Journal of Industrial Organization , 53: 63-98, July 2017. [DOI link]
We model a spectrum auction where firms purchase units to participate in a constrained, multi-product, downstream market. We use dynamic programming techniques to numerically solve for the optimal bidding strategy in a clock auction. Firms value constraining competitor market power, so incumbents often bid aggressively to shut out entrants. We find that high cost firms may hold up the market, so the auction may be inefficient and generate zero revenue. An auction may be optimal for a regulator maximising total surplus. A regulator maximising auction revenue sets reserve prices high enough to restrict spectra sold, effectively behaving as a monopolist.

Working Papers

Nonlinear Pricing, Biased Consumers, and Regulatory Policy paper
Price schedules with increasing block tariffs have been used by policy makers to regulate important markets such as water and electricity. Although such tariffs are argued to promote equity, resource conservation, and revenue stability, they are usually not argued to promote efficiency. This paper re-examines regulated non-linear pricing in light of recent evidence regarding how consumers respond to complex price schedules. Ito (2014) shows that, when confronted with complex price schedules, electricity customers respond to changes in average price (AP) rather than to changes in marginal price (MP). I find that optimal regulated non-linear pricing under AP response behavior is independent of the consumer type distribution. Fixing consumer preferences and the type distribution, increasing per-unit prices may be optimal when consumers respond to AP, while decreasing per-unit prices are optimal when consumers respond to MP. These results suggest that the equity-efficiency trade-off associated with increasing block tariffs may be less severe than previously believed.
